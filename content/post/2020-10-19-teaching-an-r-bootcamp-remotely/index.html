Background

Back in Fall 2018, the Department of Psychology at the University of Oregon started to offer an introductory R Bootcamp the week before the start of the new academic year. The bootcamp consists of three 3-4 hour-long sessions that take place over the course of a week. The idea behind the bootcamp is to give a first exposure to R and programming in general to incoming students who have little to no background in these tools before being launched headfirst into having to use R to analyze data for their first year project and to complete labs and homework assignments for PSY611, the first in a series of graduate statistics courses taught entirely in R.

In addition, as Iâ€™ve written about previously, current grad students, along with other members of the psychology department, have expressed interest in developing a stronger foundation in skills like basic programming, data wrangling, and data visualization. So the bootcamp also serves as an opportunity for those who want it to have a refresher and deeper dive into those topics.

After spending lot of time thinking about how to improve the data science learning experience for my fellow grad students and having recently become an RStudio Certified Instructor in the tidyverse, it was my great privilege to be able to lead the 3rd Annual UO Psych Bootcamp this year. ğŸ¥³ However, I found myself with a rather significant challenge. The R Bootcamp, like many introductory workshops, usually happens in-person in a large room with a giant project screen, coffee + snacks, and the ability to run over and look over someoneâ€™s shoulder at their computer screen. Because of COVID-19, I found myself forced to design and deliver a bootcamp to a large group of beginners (many using R for the very first time) entirely over a webcam. Needless to say, this felt a little daunting ğŸ˜¨. Fortunately, there are many people out there who are experts in this sort of thing who have graciously shared their resources and advice, and this helped me immensely to prepare.

For anyone who is maybe new at teaching R, teaching online, or both, the goal of this post is to share with you some valuable lessons I learned and resources I used during this process to hopefully assist your own teaching experience.

Who attended?

While the majority of those who attended were incoming first-year psychology grad students, we also had a mix of grad students at all levels, undergrads, research assistants, lab managers, staff data analysts, and faculty members from a variety of departments/centers, including Psychology, Linguistics, Economics and the Center for Translational Neuroscience.

# clean data
plot_data <- registration %>% 
  janitor::clean_names() %>% 
  select(position = what_is_your_position_at_uo,
         experience = do_you_have_any_experience_in_r_no_experience_is_required) %>%
  mutate(position = factor(case_when(position %in% c("Undergraduate", "Undergraduate student") ~ "Undergrad Student",

                              TRUE ~ as.character(position))),
         experience = factor(experience, levels = c(
           "I've never heard of it",
           "I've heard of it but have never used it",
           "I've opened it before",
           "I've used it to analyze data",
           "I use it often to analyze data")))
